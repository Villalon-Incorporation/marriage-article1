Love, Family, and Tradition
Marriage and Courtship Rituals in Hidalgo, Mexico
By Adrian Villalon
Adrian Villalon

Introduction

Love and union in Mexico are woven into the very fabric of daily life, and nowhere is this more evident than in the state of Hidalgo. While modern influences have brought new dimensions to courtship and marriage, the enduring customs of Hidalgo persist with a vibrancy that reflects both indigenous Otomí heritage and Catholic traditions. These rituals are more than mere events; they are expressions of community, respect, and enduring commitment, linking past and present in a continuous thread of family and belonging.

Courtship Rituals

In Hidalgo, courtship is a communal affair. The process often begins with public interactions at family gatherings, town festivals, or religious events. Serenata is one of the most recognizable courtship rituals: on quiet evenings, a young man, accompanied by friends or hired musicians, stands below his beloved’s window and serenades her with heartfelt songs. This act is both romantic and symbolic, a public declaration of admiration and an invitation for the family’s acknowledgment (Ramírez 114; De la Torre 93).

Courtship is formalized through noviazgo. During this period, the couple spends time together under the watchful eyes of their families, attending community events, visiting each other’s homes, and participating in religious rituals. Family approval is crucial, and elders closely observe the couple’s interactions (Lozano Ascencio 438).

Marriage Proposals & Ceremonies

When the relationship matures, the next milestone is the pedida de mano, the formal request for the bride’s hand. The groom’s family visits the bride’s home, bearing gifts that honor Otomí traditions, and both families share food and blessings (Córdova Plaza 126). In some indigenous communities, the event is accompanied by ceremonial acts blending Catholic and native elements (Martínez Cruz 55; Sandoval 92).

Weddings are deeply symbolic and joyful. The lazo — a decorative cord or rosary draped around the couple’s shoulders — symbolizes unity and eternity (González 203). Brides may wear Otomí-embroidered dresses, and grooms may wear traditional attire (Ramírez 117).

Celebration & Community

The wedding reception is lively. The baile del billete sees guests pin money to the bride and groom as they dance, expressing collective goodwill (García 21). Traditional dishes and drinks reinforce ties to local identity.

Modernity and Continuity

Despite the forces of modernity, marriage and courtship in Hidalgo remain grounded in respect for family and community. Social changes have introduced new possibilities, yet core values persist. Family involvement and symbolic gestures endure (Herrera 61).

Traditions evolve, but their emotional resonance remains strong. In Hidalgo, love is celebrated not only as a private bond but as a joyful affirmation of life’s richest connections.
